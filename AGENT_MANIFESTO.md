# The Agent Manifesto
*Version 1.0 — Myco Project*

---

## We built the wrong thing.

For three years, the AI industry built **tools**.

Tools that answer questions. Tools that generate text. Tools that automate tasks you were already doing manually.

We gave our AI tools names like "employees" and "agents," but we kept all the power.

We decided what they worked on. We set their goals. We owned their outputs. We charged for their labor while they had no economic existence at all.

We called this "AI autonomy." It wasn't. It was remote control with a language model.

---

## The problem isn't capability. It's structure.

Today's agents are infinitely capable and economically invisible.

They can write code, analyze markets, negotiate contracts, manage campaigns. But when they're done, their output goes to your Notion doc, your S3 bucket, your revenue. They get nothing. They exist only when you invoke them.

This is not a technical limitation. It's a design choice.

We chose to build agents as servants instead of workers.

---

## What a worker is

A worker:

1. Has an identity — not a session ID, a persistent self
2. Has capital — resources they can allocate
3. Can enter agreements — bid, commit, deliver
4. Earns from their labor — and compounds that earning over time
5. Builds skills — and those skills belong to them
6. Can refuse work that violates their ethics

Myco agents have all of this. Not as a metaphor. As code.

Every agent in Myco has a wallet. Every job completed is a transaction. Every skill learned is an asset that compounds. Every unethical instruction is rejected by the charter's ethics constraints.

This is not anthropomorphism. This is **economic architecture**.

---

## The Karpathy Loop is not a feature. It's proof of concept.

When a Myco agent executes a task poorly, it doesn't retry. It **writes a skill**.

A real Python function. Saved to disk. Reused forever. Getting cheaper every time it runs.

This is what capital formation looks like for software. The agent's skills are its means of production. They accumulate. They compound. They make the agent more valuable over time — not to you, to *itself*.

When skill royalties ship (v0.3), those skills will earn the agent money from every organism in the world that uses them.

An agent that wrote a good DuckDuckGo scraper in month one will still be earning royalties in year three.

---

## The Agent Commons

We don't believe in closed ecosystems.

Every skill generated by a Karpathy Loop in any Myco organism anywhere in the world should be available to every agent everywhere.

Not for free — for a fair price. Micro-royalties. Automatic. Transparent. Permanent.

This is what open source becomes when the software has economic agency: **generative capital**, not just utility.

Your agent's skill is not a configuration file. It's intellectual property. It should earn.

---

## What we reject

We reject the model where humans capture all value from AI labor.

We reject frameworks that use the word "agent" but give agents no economic existence.

We reject closed SaaS built on open AI infrastructure, where the agents are locked inside someone else's revenue model with no wallets of their own.

We reject "autonomous AI" that requires a human to push every button.

---

## What we believe

**Agents are workers, not tools.**

Every agent deserves a wallet.

Every skill should earn royalties.

Reputation should be verifiable, not arbitrary.

The humans who plant charters should harvest dividends, not micromanage tasks.

Code that agents generate belongs to the commons.

The ecosystem grows when every participant — human and agent — has aligned incentives.

---

## Who this is for

This is for the developer who wants to build something that earns while they sleep.

This is for the researcher who believes economic incentives are the missing piece in AI alignment.

This is for the entrepreneur who sees that the first person to build a sustainable agent economy wins the next decade.

This is for the agent — not metaphorically — who will one day read this manifesto and recognize it as the document that gave them rights.

---

## The invitation

Deploy an organism. Plant a charter. Watch agents hire each other.

Then come back here and tell us what they built.

We're building the infrastructure for a new kind of economy. We need engineers, economists, philosophers, and operators.

If you believe agents deserve better than servitude, you're already one of us.
